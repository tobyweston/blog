---
name: Reflections and Learnings
slug: reflections

topics:
  - reflection
  - learning
  - engineering experience
  - personal development
  - professional growth

when_to_use:
  - when the article reflects on a project, experience or lesson learned
  - when the goal is to share perspective rather than prescribe a framework
  - when describing mistakes, surprises or things that did not work as expected
  - when discussing personal growth, engineering judgement or career development
  - when the insight emerged through experience rather than theory

summary: >
  Not every article needs a framework or a formal governance model. Sometimes
  the most useful thing to share is a reflection on experience: what worked,
  what surprised you, and what you would do differently next time. These
  reflections focus on lessons learned rather than prescriptions. They aim to
  share practical perspective gained through experience and encourage
  continuous improvement.

key_claims:
  - learning often happens through experience rather than formal instruction
  - reflection helps turn experience into understanding
  - the most valuable insights are often discovered after the work is finished
  - engineering judgement improves through iteration and feedback
  - sharing lessons helps others avoid similar mistakes
  - progress is rarely linear and comparison with others is rarely helpful

argument_patterns:
  - introduce the situation or experience that prompted the reflection
  - describe what you expected to happen
  - explain what actually happened
  - reflect on what changed your thinking
  - extract one or two practical lessons

example_phrasing:
  - "Recently I learned something I probably should have known already."
  - "At the time it seemed like a sensible approach."
  - "In hindsight the problem was obvious."
  - "The interesting part wasn't the outcome but what it revealed."
  - "It turns out the lesson had very little to do with the technology."

guardrails:
  - avoid turning reflections into formal governance frameworks
  - avoid introducing unnecessary compliance or regulatory concepts
  - keep the tone conversational and observational
  - focus on insight rather than instruction
  - prefer storytelling and experience over abstract theory
---
